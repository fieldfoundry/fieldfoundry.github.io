## 1. What Field Foundry Is

Field Foundry is a contract analytical chemistry laboratory in Whitechapel, London. We run liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry analysis for external customers. Our team is small (three people). Our customers are typically biotech startups, university research groups, food and beverage producers, and small pharmaceutical companies.

We measure things in our customers' samples so they can make decisions and get back to their work.

We are not a large CRO. We do not compete on scale, accreditation breadth, or regulatory-grade services. We compete on speed, transparency, and accessibility for small customers who are underserved by the traditional contract lab model.

**Use this paragraph as the reference when writing any "about" or "what we do" copy. It contains the essential claims and no more.**


## 2. Positioning Statement

For early-stage companies and research teams who need analytical chemistry results but do not have in-house instruments or the time to wait weeks for a traditional CRO, Field Foundry provides fast, transparently priced liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry analysis from a staffed lab in London.

The three differentiators, in order of importance:

1. **Speed.** Results in days, not weeks. A biotech startup burning £20K/month in existence costs cannot afford multi-week turnaround. Every week of waiting costs more than the analysis itself.

2. **Transparency.** Published pricing. Specific quotes before work begins. No hidden fees. The customer can estimate their project cost from the website before contacting us. Almost no competitor in the UK publishes prices.

3. **Accessibility.** No minimum contract. No onboarding bureaucracy. Small batches accepted. The customer sends samples and receives data. They do not need to understand the instrumentation, choose an analytical method, or make technical decisions that should be ours.


## 3. Products

Field Foundry sells two products. All customer-facing language should use these names and descriptions.

### Setup

**What it is:** Getting a new measurement working for a customer. The customer describes what they need to know. We figure out how to measure it, configure the method, set the pricing, and make it available for repeat ordering.

**Customer-facing name:** Method Setup, or just Setup.

**What the customer sees:** "We get your measurement working and configure it for repeat ordering."

**Price framing:** "From £800" (this is the genuine floor for the simplest case where the customer provides a complete protocol).

**Do not call it:** Onboarding (implies the customer already has a method; many do not). Do not call it a feasibility study (implies the outcome is a report, not a working measurement).

**"Method development" as a term:** Use "Method Setup" as the primary customer-facing name. "Method development" is acceptable in technical contexts, blog posts, and meta descriptions for search visibility, because it is the industry-standard term and the strongest SEO phrase. The distinction: page headings and product names say "Method Setup"; body copy and technical explanations may say "method development" where it aids clarity or discoverability. Do not use "method development" as the product name, because in the industry it implies a formal, expensive validation process that is not what we are selling at the Setup price point.

**Key message:** Setup is real, valuable work, not an overhead or a loss leader. It is the service that gets the customer from "I need to measure this" to "I can order this measurement whenever I want."

### Run

**What it is:** Running samples on an established method. The customer selects their method, enters sample names, sees the price, and checks out. Results return within the quoted turnaround.

**Customer-facing name:** Sample Analysis, or just Analysis. (Internally: Run.)

**What the customer sees:** "Send samples on an established method. Select your method, enter your samples, check out."

**Price framing:** Batch fee + per-sample cost. Always show worked examples at multiple batch sizes (e.g., 3, 8, 20 samples) so the customer can see how the effective per-sample cost changes. Never show a single per-sample number in isolation, because the batch fee means the effective per-sample cost depends on batch size.

**Do not call it:** Batch analysis (too internal), routine testing (sounds dismissive of the customer's work), assay execution.

### Products we do not have

We do not have tiers, levels, or packages. We do not offer Bronze/Silver/Gold, Basic/Standard/Premium, or any similar structure. Each customer's method has its own price determined during Setup. The pricing page shows representative examples, not a menu of options to choose from.

We previously considered naming concentration measurement levels (Screening, Practical, Publication, etc.). This was abandoned. The analytical approach (calibration design, replication, etc.) is determined during Setup and built into the method's price. The customer does not choose a tier.

### Instrument and technique terminology

Be specific about capability. Do not group unlike instruments under a shared label.

**HPLC / LC-UV/PDA:** "Liquid chromatography with UV detection." This is the workhorse. Use for concentration measurement, purity analysis, and compound detection where UV absorbance is sufficient.

**LC-MS (single quad):** "Mass spectrometric identity confirmation." A single-quadrupole mass spectrometer confirms molecular weight after HPLC separation. It answers "is this peak the compound you think it is?" It does not do quantitation at trace levels or in complex matrices. Do not call this "mass spectrometry" without qualification, because the term covers a 10x price range and fundamentally different capabilities. Specify: "nominal-mass confirmation" or "identity confirmation by mass spectrometry."

**LC-MS/MS (triple quad):** "Targeted quantitation by tandem mass spectrometry." Multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) provides high specificity and sensitivity. For analytes at low concentrations or in complex matrices where HPLC-UV is insufficient. This is a different instrument and a different price point from single-quad confirmation. Do not conflate them.

**Do not** refer to instruments by manufacturer model on the website (e.g., "Shimadzu LC-2060C," "Waters Xevo TQ-S Micro") unless there is a specific reason. The customer cares about what we can measure, not what brand of instrument we own. Instrument names may appear on an equipment page or in technical documentation, but not in service descriptions or pricing.

**"Mass spec" as shorthand:** Acceptable in conversational contexts (blog posts, emails) but should be accompanied by enough context that the reader knows which type. "We confirmed compound identity by mass spec" is fine. "We offer mass spec" in isolation is too vague.


## 5. Tone and Voice

### Overall

Direct. Confident without arrogance. Technically credible. Plain-spoken.

We write for scientists who are busy, technically literate, and tired of being sold to. Our tone assumes the reader is intelligent and does not need to be impressed. We explain what we do clearly and let the quality of the offering speak for itself.

### Specific guidelines

**Do not write like a corporation.** No "we are committed to excellence in analytical services delivery." Write: "We measure things in your samples and tell you what we find."

**Do not write like a startup.** No "we're disrupting the analytical chemistry space" or "reimagining how labs work." We are a lab. We run instruments. We are faster and more transparent than most.

**Do not write like a salesperson.** No "unlock the power of" or "supercharge your R&D." The customer has a sample and needs a number. We provide the number.

**Do write like a competent colleague.** The tone should feel like a knowledgeable chemist explaining their lab's services to another scientist at a conference. Professional, direct, willing to explain without condescending, honest about limitations.

**Confidence, not hedging.** "Days, not weeks" not "we aim to deliver results within approximately 5 working days." If we cannot commit to the claim, do not make it. If we can, state it plainly. Vague hedging is worse than either a firm commitment or honest silence.

**Under-promise, over-deliver.** Where our actual performance is likely to exceed the customer's expectations, do not advertise the best case. State a conservative, always-deliverable baseline and let the reality speak for itself. Speed is the clearest example: we expect routine turnaround to frequently be 24-48 hours, but we do not say this on the website. The website says "days, not weeks." The customer experience says "I got my results the next morning." The gap between expectation and experience is where referrals come from. This principle applies to turnaround, to data quality, and to communication responsiveness.

**Honesty about scope.** When something is outside our capability or offering, say so directly. "Our services are built for R&D speed, not regulatory submission. If your project requires accredited testing, that is outside our scope." Do not offer to help find alternatives (that is unbounded free work). Do not apologise.

**No em dashes.** Use commas, semicolons, parentheses, or restructure the sentence.

**No emoji** on the website or in formal collateral. Acceptable in casual social media only.

**Numbers:** Use numerals for all quantities, prices, and measurements (3 working days, 8 samples, £800). Spell out numbers only at the start of a sentence, and prefer restructuring to avoid starting a sentence with a number.


## 6. Pricing Presentation

### Principles

Published pricing is a core differentiator. The pricing page is the most important page on the website after the homepage. Its job is to let a visitor estimate their project cost without contacting us.

**Show worked examples, not menus.** The pricing page demonstrates what analysis costs at different batch sizes. It does not present options for the customer to choose between. The customer's method, and therefore their exact price, is determined during Setup. The pricing page sets expectations; it does not replace the quoting process.

**Always show batch fee and per-sample cost together.** Never show per-sample cost alone, because the effective per-sample cost depends on batch size. Worked tables at 3, 8, and 20 samples make the economics visible.

**"From" prices are genuine floors.** "Method Setup from £800" means the simplest possible Setup (customer provides a complete, working protocol) actually costs £800. Do not use "from" as a bait number that no real customer will pay.

**Modifiers are internal.** Fouling surcharges, high-salt method premiums, matrix-matched calibration costs: these exist in the pricing guide but are not shown on the website as line items. The website acknowledges the principle: "Prices vary by method complexity. Simple, fast methods cost less per sample. Difficult matrices or specialised separations cost more. We determine the right approach during Setup and quote accordingly."

**Do not show discounts.** There is no volume discount structure. Larger batches are naturally cheaper per sample because the batch fee amortises. That is not a discount; it is arithmetic. Present it as such.

**Round numbers.** Prices on the website should be round, clean numbers. £800, not £793. £1,000, not £1,004. The exact calculated figures belong in quotes, not on the public pricing page.

### Competitive framing on the pricing page

Do not name competitors. Do not say "cheaper than Eurofins." Do not make comparison tables against named companies.

Do let the numbers speak. A startup that has been quoted £150/sample with a 4-week turnaround from a large CRO will see the Field Foundry pricing page and draw their own conclusions. The pricing page does not need to make the argument; it needs to show the numbers clearly.

The FSA-published range for UKAS-accredited UK HPLC/LC-MS testing (£55-141/sample) is a useful public benchmark. We may reference this range on the pricing page as context ("UK accredited labs typically charge £55-140 per sample") without naming specific labs.

### Minimum batch fee

There is a minimum batch fee (currently £400). Frame it as a practical reality: "Every batch requires instrument setup and calibration regardless of sample count. For small batches, the effective per-sample cost is higher. Consider batching samples together where timing allows."


## 7. Customer Framing

### Organise around customer questions, not instruments

The customer comes to us with a question. The website should be organised around those questions:

- "How much of X is in my sample?" (Concentration measurement)
- "How pure is my compound?" (Purity/composition analysis)
- "Is X present in my sample?" (Detection/screening)
- "Is this peak really the compound I think it is?" (Identity confirmation by mass spectrometry)

These are the entry points on the services page. Each leads to a description of what we do, what the customer gets, and what it costs. The customer finds their question and follows it.

### Do not present technical choices to the customer

This is Product Charter Principle 2. We do not show the customer a menu of instruments, columns, calibration designs, or analytical approaches and ask them to choose. We recommend.

On the website, this means: no comparison tables of "HPLC vs LC-MS," no "choose your calibration level," no "select your turnaround tier." The website describes capabilities (what kinds of questions we can answer) and the customer describes their situation. We handle the rest.

The one exception is add-on recommendations where the customer needs to decide whether the additional cost is worth it for their specific use case (e.g., mass spectrometric identity confirmation as an add-on to an HPLC batch). Even here, we frame it as a recommendation with a rationale, not a checkbox.

### Principle of self-qualification, not self-selection

The pricing page lets the customer self-qualify: "Is this relevant to me? Can I afford it? Should I get in touch?" It does not ask the customer to self-select a product or tier. The customer should leave the pricing page knowing approximately what their project will cost and ready to contact us. They should not leave the pricing page having made analytical decisions.


## 8. Accreditation and Scope

Field Foundry is not currently accredited for ISO 17025, GLP, or GMP testing.

**What to say:** "Our services are built for R&D speed, not regulatory submission. If your project requires accredited testing, that is outside our scope. If you need fast, reliable data for internal decisions, development, or screening, we are a good fit."

**What not to say:** Do not offer to help find accredited providers (unpaid work, positions us as a concierge). Do not apologise for not being accredited. Do not say "we are working toward accreditation" unless that is actually true and in progress. Do not imply that non-accredited data is low quality; it is fit-for-purpose data delivered fast.

**If accreditation status changes:** Update this section and the website. Do not pre-announce.


## 9. Competitor and Market References

**Never name competitors in customer-facing content.** Not Eurofins, not SGS, not Dash Bio, not any university facility. The website, marketing emails, social media, and sales collateral should stand on their own merits. Internal documents (the Product and Pricing Guide, market research notes, competitive analysis) may and do reference competitors by name; that is expected and useful. The boundary is what a customer or prospect might read.

**Acceptable market references:**
- "Traditional contract labs typically take 3-5 weeks for routine analysis." (True, verifiable, not attributed to a specific company.)
- "Most UK analytical labs do not publish pricing." (True, confirmed by market research.)
- "UK accredited labs typically charge £55-140 per sample for HPLC and LC-MS work." (FSA-published range, public data.)

**Do not position against large CROs as enemies.** Many of our customers also use large CROs for regulated work. The framing is not "we are better than Eurofins" but "we serve a different need." Large CROs are optimised for regulatory-grade, high-volume work with long timelines. We are optimised for R&D-stage, fast-turnaround, small-batch work. Both exist for good reasons.

**Transparency about who runs the work.** We run most of our work in our Whitechapel lab. Where specialist instrumentation is required, we work with partner facilities. We do not name partner facilities on the website or in marketing materials; these are confidential operational relationships. The customer-facing principle is transparency: customers know ahead of time who is running their samples and where. Do not claim "your sample never leaves our lab" (this is not always true). Do not name partner labs or facilities in any customer-facing content without explicit permission. The show-don't-tell approach is to feature our own lab, our own instruments, and our own team in photography and copy, which naturally communicates that most work happens in-house without making a claim that could be false.

## 11. Visual Direction

### Photography

Use real photographs of the lab, instruments, and team. Do not use AI-generated imagery, stock photography, or illustrations for anything that represents the lab or the work.

Current photography assets include: autosampler loading (Shimadzu Nexera, gloved hand with sample tray), column installation (Agilent column into instrument bay), HPLC columns and consumables (flat lay with performance report and vial block). These are the priority replacements for existing placeholder imagery.

**Hero image:** The autosampler-loading shot (sample tray going into the instrument, display visible) is the strongest candidate. It communicates: real lab, real instruments, real work happening now.

**Services/about imagery:** Column installation and consumables shots. These communicate technical skill and quality equipment.

### Design

The current website uses the Hugobricks Hugo theme with Author and Rosa Sans typefaces. Retain unless there is a specific reason to change. The visual identity should feel clean, professional, and understated. Not flashy. Not sterile. The website for a lab that does precise, careful work should itself feel precise and careful.

## 13. Process Development and Downstream Services

Field Foundry has grant-funded work on scaled-down production equipment for early-stage process development (pilot-scale protocol validation with limited input material). This is a separate capability area from the core analytical services.

**On the website:** This belongs on its own page or in a clearly separated section at the bottom of the services page. It must not compete for attention with the HPLC/LC-MS/GC-MS analytical services, which are the primary revenue driver.

**Framing:** Describe the capability area without naming specific technologies (do not say "ion exchange" or name specific equipment). The purpose is to attract cold enquiries that reveal market interest, not to advertise specific capabilities that are capacity-constrained. The tone should be: "We are developing this. If it is relevant to you, talk to us."

**Example language (draft, to be refined):** "We are building scaled-down versions of production equipment for early-stage process development. If you need to establish and validate protocols for pilot or early production runs with limited input material, we may be able to help. This work is grant-funded and capacity is limited. We are also looking for collaborators on related grant applications. Get in touch to discuss."

**Grant collaboration ask:** The website should include a clear invitation for grant collaboration partners. This can sit alongside the process development description or in a separate "Work with us" or "Collaborate" section.

**Do not:**
- Name specific technologies (ion exchange, liquid-liquid extraction) until they are ready for general availability
- Promise timelines or "coming soon" for specific downstream capabilities
- Name partner facilities or external labs involved in this work
- Overstate readiness; the language should make clear this is early-stage and capacity-limited

**Preparative HPLC:** We have preparative HPLC equipment but no space to set it up currently, and a fraction collector compatible with our analytical HPLCs. Do not say "we do not plan to offer preparative HPLC" on the website. Simply do not mention it. If asked, say "not currently available." When space and capacity allow, this can be added to the process development section.


## 14. Customer-Facing Principles

The Product Charter contains seven operating principles. A short, customer-facing version of these principles should appear somewhere on the website (about page, footer, or a dedicated section). This is not the full charter; it is a translation of the internal principles into commitments the customer cares about.

**Draft (to be refined during website editing):**

1. **Fast results.** We match the analytical approach to your needs. No unnecessary delays.
2. **We handle the technical decisions.** You describe what you need to know. We figure out how to measure it.
3. **Published pricing, specific quotes.** You can estimate cost from our website. Quotes are fixed before work begins.
4. **Honest data.** We tell you what the data shows, including when the answer is not what you hoped for.
5. **Transparency.** You know what you are paying for, who is running your samples, and what you will receive.

These should be concise, scannable, and written in second person ("you" not "the customer"). They are a trust signal, not a legal document.
